---
title: Aloe Vera
scientific_name: Aloe barbadensis miller
common_names:
  - Medicinal Aloe
  - Burn Plant
toxicity_level: ⚠️ MODERATE TOXICITY – GASTROINTESTINAL DISTRESS
summary: >-
  While aloe gel is safe for human use, the latex (yellow sap near the leaf
  skin) contains saponins and anthraquinones that are toxic to cats. Ingestion
  can cause vomiting, diarrhea, and lethargy.
ascpa_link: >-
  https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/animal-poison-control/toxic-and-non-toxic-plants/aloe
image: >-
  /uploads/7ae0aff1-4b60-4c05-aa34-fcd6a9ea3dd2_7930717a90c33c714f1ae8d742554593_ComfyUI_033fc57d_00001_.png
symptoms:
  - Vomiting
  - Diarrhea
  - Lethargy
  - Tremors (in rare cases)
  - Dehydration due to fluid loss
what_to_do: "**\U0001F6A8 Contact your vet if your cat ate aloe vera.**\n\n1. Remove any remaining plant material from your cat’s mouth.\n2. Call your veterinarian or the **ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center** at **(888) 426-4435**.\n3. Monitor for signs of dehydration (dry gums, sunken eyes).\n4. Do not induce vomiting unless instructed by a professional.\n\n> \U0001F4A1 Note: The clear inner gel is low-risk, but cats usually chew the whole leaf—including the toxic outer layer.\n"
safe_alternatives:
  - Calathea (Prayer Plant)
  - Boston Fern
  - Parlor Palm
  - Peperomia
  - Haworthia (a non-toxic succulent lookalike)
sources:
