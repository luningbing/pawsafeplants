---
title: Tulips
scientific_name: Tulipa spp.
common_names:
  - Garden Tulip
  - Spring Tulip
toxicity_level: ⚠️ LOW TO MODERATE TOXICITY – BULBS ARE MOST DANGEROUS
summary: >-
  Tulips contain tulipalin A and B, which are most concentrated in the bulbs.
  Ingestion can cause drooling, vomiting, and diarrhea. Leaves and flowers are
  less toxic but still irritating.
ascpa_link: >-
  https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/animal-poison-control/toxic-and-non-toxic-plants/tulip
image: >-
  https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1520302618106-4e2f8dc772b1?ixlib=rb-4.0.3&w=800&h=600&fit=crop
symptoms:
  - Drooling
  - Vomiting
  - Diarrhea
  - Mouth irritation
  - Abdominal pain
what_to_do: "**Low risk from petals—but bulbs = high risk.**\n\n1. If your cat chewed a flower: rinse mouth, monitor.\n2. If bulbs were ingested: **call vet immediately**.\n3. Keep potted tulips or cut bouquets on high surfaces.\n4. Never compost tulip bulbs where pets can access.\n\n> \U0001F4A1 Spring tip: Easter and Mother’s Day bouquets often include tulips—check before gifting!\n"
safe_alternatives:
  - Carnations (non-toxic in small amounts)
  - Sunflowers
  - Gerbera Daisies
sources:
  - ASPCA Toxic and Non-Toxic Plants Database
  - Veterinary Clinics of North America
---
